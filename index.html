1. The QCD Lagrangian and the Chiral Limit

Quantum Chromodynamics (QCD) is the non-Abelian $SU(3)_c$ gauge theory describing the strong interaction. The Lagrangian density for $N_f$ flavors of quarks is given by:

$$\mathcal{L}_{QCD} = -\frac{1}{4}G_{\mu\nu}^a G^{\mu\nu}_a + \sum_{f=1}^{N_f} \bar{q}_f (i\gamma^\mu D_{\mu} - m_f) q_f$$

We restrict our attention to the light quark sector, specifically $N_f = 3$ ($u, d, s$). Their physical masses are small compared to the intrinsic QCD scale $\Lambda_{QCD} \approx 250 \text{ MeV}$. We can therefore study the chiral limit where the mass matrix $M \to 0$.

Using the chiral projectors $P_{R,L} = \frac{1 \pm \gamma_5}{2}$, we decompose the Dirac spinors: $q = q_R + q_L$. The massless Lagrangian separates perfectly into right-handed and left-handed sectors:

$$\mathcal{L}_{m=0} = \bar{q}_L (i \not \! D) q_L + \bar{q}_R (i \not \! D) q_R$$

Because there is no mass term mixing them, this Lagrangian is invariant under independent global unitary transformations of the left and right fields, revealing the classical global $U(3)_L \times U(3)_R$ chiral symmetry.

2. The Chiral Algebra and Currents

We decompose the $U(3) \times U(3)$ group into $SU(3)_V \times SU(3)_A \times U(1)_V \times U(1)_A$ by defining Vector ($V = R + L$) and Axial-Vector ($A = R - L$) transformations.

The corresponding conserved charges form the chiral $SU(3) \times SU(3)$ Lie algebra under equal-time commutators:

$$[Q_V^a, Q_V^b] = i f^{abc} Q_V^c$$ $$[Q_V^a, Q_A^b] = i f^{abc} Q_A^c$$ $$[Q_A^a, Q_A^b] = i f^{abc} Q_V^c$$

Finally, we have the singlet currents for $U(1)_V$ and $U(1)_A$:

$$V^\mu = \bar{q} \gamma^\mu q, \quad A^\mu = \bar{q} \gamma^\mu \gamma_5 q$$

3. The Exact Symmetry: $U(1)_V$ and Baryon Conservation

While the axial symmetries govern the complex mass spectrum of mesons, the $U(1)_V$ symmetry does something arguably more important: it ensures the stability of matter.

The Vector Transformation

The $U(1)_V$ subgroup corresponds to applying the exact same phase transformation to both left-handed and right-handed fields simultaneously: $L = R = e^{i\alpha}$. The Dirac spinor transforms globally as:

$$q(x) \rightarrow e^{i\alpha} q(x), \quad \bar{q}(x) \rightarrow \bar{q}(x) e^{-i\alpha}$$

Perfect Classical Invariance

Unlike the axial transformations ($e^{i\alpha\gamma_5}$), which flip signs when pulled through the $\gamma_0$ matrix and cause the mass term to break the symmetry, the pure vector phase commutes with everything. The kinetic and mass terms are perfectly invariant:

$$m \bar{q} q \rightarrow m (\bar{q} e^{-i\alpha}) (e^{i\alpha} q) = m \bar{q} q$$

This means $U(1)_V$ is an exact classical symmetry of the Lagrangian, regardless of whether the quarks are massless or massive.

The Noether Current and Baryon Number

By Noether's Theorem, this continuous symmetry corresponds to a strictly conserved vector current $V^\mu = \bar{q} \gamma^\mu q$. The conserved charge is the spatial integral of the zeroth component (the number operator):

$$Q_V = \int d^3x \, q^\dagger q = N_q - N_{\bar{q}}$$

This operator counts the total number of quarks minus antiquarks. We define the Baryon Number ($B$) as $B = \frac{1}{3} (N_q - N_{\bar{q}})$.

Surviving the Quantum Realm

Does $U(1)_V$ suffer a quantum anomaly like $U(1)_A$? No. The Fujikawa Jacobian for a pure vector transformation evaluates to exactly 1. Because there is no $\gamma_5$ matrix to force a non-zero trace with the gluon field strength tensors, the anomaly trace vanishes entirely. The conservation is exact at the quantum level: $\partial_\mu V^\mu = 0$.

Phenomenology: Proton Stability

Because $U(1)_V$ is an exact quantum symmetry of QCD, Baryon Number is strictly conserved. This is why the proton is stable. If $U(1)_V$ were broken, the proton could decay into lighter particles (e.g., $p^+ \rightarrow e^+ + \pi^0$). The exactness of this symmetry is the structural foundation of all stable atomic matter.

4. The Fate of the Symmetries

This massive symmetry group is shattered in entirely distinct ways, dictating low-energy hadron physics:

1. The Exact Symmetry: $U(1)_V$
Remains perfectly unbroken, guaranteeing the conservation of Baryon Number.
2. Explicit Breaking: Quark Masses
Because $m_u, m_d, m_s \neq 0$, the $SU(3)_A$ symmetry is explicitly broken at the Lagrangian level, making the resulting Goldstone bosons massive (pseudo-Goldstone bosons).
3. Spontaneous Breaking: $SU(3)_L \times SU(3)_R \to SU(3)_V$
The strong force causes the vacuum to condense, breaking the axial generators and preserving only the vector flavor symmetry. This generates the 8 light pseudo-scalar mesons.
4. Anomalous Breaking: $U(1)_A$
Explicitly broken by the quantum mechanics of the path integral measure. It does not produce a light Goldstone boson, resulting in the heavy $\eta^\prime$ meson mass ($958 \text{ MeV}$).

5. Spontaneous Symmetry Breaking and the Vacuum

The non-zero vacuum expectation value (VEV) is the chiral condensate:

$$\langle 0 | \bar{q}_i q_j | 0 \rangle = \langle 0 | \bar{q}_{R,i} q_{L,j} + \bar{q}_{L,i} q_{R,j} | 0 \rangle = - \Sigma \, \delta_{ij}$$

Where $\Sigma \approx (250 \text{ MeV})^3$. According to Goldstone's Theorem, for every broken generator, a massless scalar particle appears. There are $8$ broken axial generators, resulting in an octet of Goldstone bosons.

6. Chiral Perturbation Theory (ChPT)

We parameterize the Goldstone bosons as phase fluctuations of the chiral condensate. We define a unitary $3 \times 3$ matrix field $U(x) \in SU(3)$:

$$U(x) = \exp\left( i \frac{\sqrt{2}}{f_\pi} \Phi(x) \right), \quad \Phi(x) = \sum_{a=1}^8 \frac{\lambda^a}{\sqrt{2}} \pi^a(x)$$

The leading order $\mathcal{O}(p^2)$ term is the non-linear sigma model:

$$\mathcal{L}_{eff}^{(2)} = \frac{f_\pi^2}{4} \text{Tr}\left( \partial_\mu U \partial^\mu U^\dagger \right)$$

7. Explicit Breaking and the GMOR Relation

The leading-order explicit symmetry breaking term in the ChPT Lagrangian is:

$$\mathcal{L}_{mass}^{(2)} = \frac{f_\pi^2}{4} 2B_0 \text{Tr}\left( M U^\dagger + U M^\dagger \right)$$

Here, $B_0$ is a low-energy constant related to the condensate: $\Sigma = f_\pi^2 B_0$. Expanding this yields the Gell-Mann-Oakes-Renner (GMOR) relations:

$$m_\pi^2 = 2 B_0 \hat{m} = \frac{-\langle \bar{u}u + \bar{d}d \rangle}{2f_\pi^2} (m_u + m_d)$$

8. The $U(1)_A$ Anomaly via Fujikawa's Method

Historical Context: The $U(1)$ Problem
Before the discovery of the quantum anomaly, particle physics was plagued by a massive theoretical crisis. If the classical $U(1)_A$ symmetry is assumed to be exact, its spontaneous breaking by the chiral condensate mathematically necessitates a massless flavor-singlet Goldstone boson (the $\eta^\prime$). Even when factoring in explicit mass breaking, Steven Weinberg calculated a strict upper bound: $m_{\eta^\prime} \le \sqrt{3} m_\pi \approx 240 \text{ MeV}$.

Experimentalists, however, found the $\eta^\prime$ weighed a massive $958 \text{ MeV}$. This glaring contradiction meant either Goldstone's Theorem was wrong, or the fundamental $U(1)_A$ symmetry was an illusion. The discovery of the topological anomaly proved the latter, saving the mathematical foundations of QFT.

While the $SU(3)_A$ symmetry is broken spontaneously, the singlet $U(1)_A$ symmetry is shattered entirely by quantum effects. Kazuo Fujikawa (1979) elegantly proved that this anomaly arises strictly from the non-invariance of the path integral measure.

The Path Integral Measure

We start with the Euclidean path integral for a massless Dirac fermion. The Hermitian Euclidean Dirac operator $\not \! D = \gamma_\mu D_\mu$ has a complete set of orthonormal eigenfunctions $\phi_n(x)$ with real eigenvalues $\lambda_n$. We expand the quantum fields in terms of these eigenfunctions using Grassmann coefficients $a_n$ and $\bar{b}_n$:

$$\psi(x) = \sum_n a_n \phi_n(x), \quad \bar{\psi}(x) = \sum_n \bar{b}_n \phi_n^\dagger(x)$$

The functional measure is the product of all these Grassmann differentials: $\mathcal{D}\bar{\psi} \mathcal{D}\psi = \prod_n d\bar{b}_n da_n$.

The Chiral Transformation and the Jacobian

Under an infinitesimal local chiral transformation $\psi \rightarrow (1 + i\alpha(x)\gamma_5)\psi$, the Grassmann coefficients transform via a matrix $C_{nm}$. Because they are anti-commuting numbers, the Jacobian $\mathcal{J}$ of the measure is the inverse determinant of this transformation matrix squared:

$$\mathcal{D}\bar{\psi}' \mathcal{D}\psi' = (\det C)^{-2} \mathcal{D}\bar{\psi} \mathcal{D}\psi = \exp(-2 \text{Tr} \ln C) \mathcal{D}\bar{\psi} \mathcal{D}\psi$$

Evaluating $\text{Tr} \ln C$ yields the anomaly density $\mathcal{A}(x)$:

$$\mathcal{J} = \exp\left(-2i \int d^4x_E \, \alpha(x) \mathcal{A}(x)\right), \quad \mathcal{A}(x) = \sum_n \phi_n^\dagger(x) \gamma_5 \phi_n(x)$$

Heat Kernel Regularization

The sum over all states in $\mathcal{A}(x)$ is formally infinite. Fujikawa regularized this sum using a gauge-invariant Gaussian cutoff (the heat kernel) with a large mass scale $M$:

$$\mathcal{A}(x) = \lim_{M \to \infty} \sum_n \phi_n^\dagger(x) \gamma_5 e^{-\lambda_n^2 / M^2} \phi_n(x) = \lim_{M \to \infty} \text{Tr} \langle x | \gamma_5 e^{-\not \! D^2/M^2} | x \rangle$$

We expand the square of the Dirac operator: $\not \! D^2 = D^2 + \frac{g}{2}\sigma_{\mu\nu}G_{\mu\nu}$, where $\sigma_{\mu\nu} = \frac{i}{2}[\gamma_\mu, \gamma_\nu]$. Moving to a continuous momentum basis to evaluate the trace:

$$\mathcal{A}(x) = \lim_{M \to \infty} \text{Tr} \int \frac{d^4k}{(2\pi)^4} e^{-ikx} \gamma_5 \exp\left( -\frac{D^2 + \frac{g}{2}\sigma_{\mu\nu}G_{\mu\nu}}{M^2} \right) e^{ikx}$$

By shifting the derivative $D_\mu \to D_\mu + ik_\mu$ and rescaling the momentum $k \to Mk$, we find that to survive the limit $M \to \infty$ and yield a non-zero Dirac trace (which requires exactly four gamma matrices with $\gamma_5$), we must pull down exactly the second-order term of the field strength expansion:

$$\mathcal{A}(x) = \text{Tr} \left( \gamma_5 \frac{1}{2!} \left(-\frac{g}{2M^2}\sigma_{\mu\nu}G_{\mu\nu}\right)^2 \right) M^4 \int \frac{d^4k}{(2\pi)^4} e^{-k^2}$$

The Final Anomaly Equation

Evaluating the standard Gaussian integral ($\frac{1}{16\pi^2}$) and the Dirac trace ($\text{Tr}(\gamma_5 \sigma_{\mu\nu} \sigma_{\rho\sigma}) = 4 \epsilon_{\mu\nu\rho\sigma}$), we arrive at the exact anomalous divergence for $N_f$ flavors. Demanding the total path integral remains invariant ($\delta S + \text{Jacobian} = 0$) gives the final anomaly equation:

$$\partial_\mu A^\mu = \frac{g^2 N_f}{32\pi^2} \epsilon^{\mu\nu\rho\sigma} G_{\mu\nu}^a G_{\rho\sigma}^a = \frac{g^2 N_f}{16\pi^2} \text{Tr}_c(G_{\mu\nu} \tilde{G}^{\mu\nu})$$

9. The Mathematics of the Instanton

The term $G_{\mu\nu}\tilde{G}^{\mu\nu}$ on the right side of the anomaly equation is a total derivative. In naive perturbation theory, its integral vanishes. However, the QCD vacuum is topologically non-trivial, characterized by integer winding numbers. To understand how the vacuum transitions between these states, we must find a classical solution to the Euclidean equations of motion: the Instanton.

The Bogomol'nyi Bound and Self-Duality

We work in 4D Euclidean spacetime. The classical Yang-Mills action $S_E$ and the topological charge $Q$ (the winding number) are given by:

$$S_E = \frac{1}{4} \int d^4x_E \, G_{\mu\nu}^a G_{\mu\nu}^a$$ $$Q = \frac{g^2}{32\pi^2} \int d^4x_E \, G_{\mu\nu}^a \tilde{G}_{\mu\nu}^a$$

We want to find a gauge field configuration that minimizes the action $S_E$ for a non-zero topological charge $Q$. We use an algebraic trick called the Bogomol'nyi bound. We start with the trivial statement that the integral of a real square is non-negative:

$$\int d^4x_E \, (G_{\mu\nu}^a \mp \tilde{G}_{\mu\nu}^a)^2 \ge 0$$

Expanding the square, we get $G^2 + \tilde{G}^2 \mp 2G\tilde{G} \ge 0$. Using the fundamental identity that the square of a tensor equals the square of its dual ($\tilde{G}_{\mu\nu}^a \tilde{G}_{\mu\nu}^a = G_{\mu\nu}^a G_{\mu\nu}^a$), this simplifies to:

$$2 \int d^4x_E \, G_{\mu\nu}^a G_{\mu\nu}^a \ge \pm 2 \int d^4x_E \, G_{\mu\nu}^a \tilde{G}_{\mu\nu}^a$$

Substitute our definitions of $S_E$ and $Q$ into this inequality:

$$8 S_E \ge \pm \frac{64\pi^2}{g^2} Q \implies S_E \ge \frac{8\pi^2 |Q|}{g^2}$$

This reveals a profound topological bound on the vacuum energy. The action reaches its absolute minimum (becoming a stable classical solution, i.e., an instanton) if and only if the inequality becomes an equality. Looking back at the squared term, this happens exactly when the field is (anti-)self-dual:

$$G_{\mu\nu}^a = \pm \tilde{G}_{\mu\nu}^a$$

The BPST Solution

In 1975, Belavin, Polyakov, Schwarz, and Tyupkin (BPST) found the exact gauge field profile satisfying this self-duality condition for $Q=1$. They embedded an $SU(2)$ subgroup inside the color group and introduced the 't Hooft symbol $\eta_{a\mu\nu}$, which maps the $SU(2)$ algebra to $SO(4)$ rotations and is manifestly self-dual:

$$\eta_{a\mu\nu} = \epsilon_{a\mu\nu4} + \delta_{a\mu}\delta_{\nu4} - \delta_{a\nu}\delta_{\mu4}$$

Using this symbol, the exact BPST instanton vector potential centered at $x_0$ with size $\rho$ is:

$$A_\mu^a(x) = \frac{2}{g} \frac{\eta_{a\mu\nu} (x - x_0)_\nu}{(x - x_0)^2 + \rho^2}$$

Calculating the non-Abelian field strength $G_{\mu\nu}^a = \partial_\mu A_\nu^a - \partial_\nu A_\mu^a + g \epsilon^{abc} A_\mu^b A_\nu^c$ yields a beautifully simple, highly localized energy density:

$$G_{\mu\nu}^a(x) = -\frac{4}{g} \frac{\rho^2 \eta_{a\mu\nu}}{((x - x_0)^2 + \rho^2)^2}$$

Because $G_{\mu\nu}^a \propto \eta_{a\mu\nu}$, the field is manifestly self-dual. Squaring this field strength and integrating over all 4D spacetime yields exactly the minimal action predicted by the Bogomol'nyi bound:

$$S_{inst} = \frac{8\pi^2}{g^2}$$

The semi-classical probability amplitude for this tunneling event to occur in the vacuum is proportional to $e^{-S_{inst}} = e^{-8\pi^2/g^2}$.

The Theta Vacuum

Because these tunneling events have a finite, non-zero probability, the true energy eigenstate of the QCD vacuum must be a Bloch-like superposition of all topological states $|n\rangle$:

$$|\theta\rangle = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} e^{in\theta} |n\rangle$$

This structure guarantees that topological transitions directly impact the physical spectrum of the theory.

10. The 't Hooft Vertex and the Mass of the $\eta^\prime$

We finally arrive at the mechanism by which the topology of the gluon field physically generates the mass of the $\eta^\prime$ meson. This occurs through the interaction between the instanton's fermion zero-modes and the chiral condensate.

Fermion Zero-Modes and Grassmann Integration

According to the Atiyah-Singer Index Theorem, an instanton gauge field background ($Q=1$) guarantees the existence of exactly one left-handed, normalizable zero-energy solution $\phi_0(x)$ to the Dirac equation for every light quark flavor $f$:

$$(i \not \! D) \phi_0^{(f)}(x) = 0$$

When computing the transition amplitude induced by an instanton via the path integral, we expand the fermion fields into their eigenmodes, where $c_0^{(f)}$ and $\bar{c}_0^{(f)}$ are the Grassmann coefficients for the zero-modes:

$$q(x) = c_0 \phi_0(x) + \sum_{n \neq 0} c_n \phi_n(x)$$

The path integral measure requires integrating over all Grassmann coefficients: $\prod_f d c_0^{(f)} d \bar{c}_0^{(f)}$. Because the integral of a Grassmann variable vanishes unless the variable is explicitly present in the integrand ($\int dc \, c = 1$), the instanton amplitude is strictly zero unless the interaction process absorbs exactly one left-handed quark and emits one right-handed quark of every single flavor simultaneously.

For $N_f = 3$ ($u, d, s$), Gerard 't Hooft demonstrated that integrating out the instanton gauge field leaves behind an effective, non-local $2N_f$-fermion interaction vertex. At low energies, this can be approximated as a local operator. Defining $Y_{ij} = \bar{q}_{R,i} q_{L,j}$ where $i,j$ are flavor indices, the operator takes the form of a determinant:

$$\mathcal{L}_{\text{'t Hooft}} = \kappa \, e^{-8\pi^2/g^2} \det_{i,j} (Y_{ij}) + \text{h.c.}$$

Expanding the Flavor Determinant

Let's rigorously expand this $3 \times 3$ flavor determinant using the Levi-Civita tensor $\epsilon_{ijk}$:

$$\det(Y) = \frac{1}{3!} \epsilon_{ijk} \epsilon_{abc} Y_{ia} Y_{jb} Y_{kc}$$

This represents a 6-fermion scattering event involving all three flavors simultaneously. To extract the mass of a meson (which is a 2-fermion state $\bar{q}q$), we must evaluate this 6-fermion operator in the physical QCD vacuum.

Closing Loops with the Chiral Condensate

The vacuum is filled with the chiral condensate. We decompose our bilinear field operator $Y_{ij}$ into its constant vacuum expectation value (VEV) and its normal-ordered quantum fluctuations (the physical mesons):

$$Y_{ij} = \langle Y_{ij} \rangle + :Y_{ij}: = -\frac{1}{2} \Sigma \delta_{ij} + :Y_{ij}:$$

To find the effective mass term (which must be quadratic in the fluctuating fields), we substitute this expansion into the determinant and isolate the terms containing exactly one fluctuating field $:Y_{kc}:$ and two VEVs. Combinatorially, there are $\binom{3}{1} = 3$ identical ways to choose one fluctuating field from the three terms, which cancels the $3!$ in the determinant definition (down to a factor of $1/2$):

$$\mathcal{L}_{mass} \propto \frac{1}{2!} \epsilon_{ijk} \epsilon_{abc} \langle Y_{ia} \rangle \langle Y_{jb} \rangle :Y_{kc}: + \text{h.c.}$$

Now, substitute the diagonal VEVs $\langle Y \rangle = -\frac{1}{2} \Sigma \delta$:

$$\mathcal{L}_{mass} \propto \frac{1}{2} \epsilon_{ijk} \epsilon_{abc} \left(-\frac{1}{2}\Sigma \delta_{ia}\right) \left(-\frac{1}{2}\Sigma \delta_{jb}\right) :Y_{kc}:$$

The Kronecker deltas force $a=i$ and $b=j$. We apply this to the second Levi-Civita tensor:

$$= \frac{\Sigma^2}{8} \epsilon_{ijk} \epsilon_{ijc} :Y_{kc}:$$

We use the fundamental tensor identity for the contraction of two Levi-Civita symbols over two indices: $\epsilon_{ijk} \epsilon_{ijc} = 2 \delta_{kc}$.

$$= \frac{\Sigma^2}{8} (2 \delta_{kc}) :Y_{kc}: = \frac{\Sigma^2}{4} :Y_{kk}:$$

Expanding the trace $:Y_{kk}: = :Y_{11} + Y_{22} + Y_{33}:$, we reveal the exact structure of the dynamically generated mass term:

$$\mathcal{L}_{mass} \propto \frac{\Sigma^2}{4} \left( \bar{u}_R u_L + \bar{d}_R d_L + \bar{s}_R s_L \right) + \text{h.c.}$$

Projecting onto the Physical Mesons

Adding the hermitian conjugate ($\bar{q}_R q_L + \bar{q}_L q_R = \bar{q}q$), the instanton-induced mass Lagrangian is completely symmetric across all three flavors:

$$\mathcal{L}_{mass} \propto \frac{\Sigma^2}{2} (\bar{u}u + \bar{d}d + \bar{s}s)$$

To see which physical mesons get this mass, we express the quark bilinears in terms of the physical meson states. The neutral octet states are $\pi^0 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{2}}(u\bar{u} - d\bar{d})$ and $\eta_8 = \frac{1}{\sqrt{6}}(u\bar{u} + d\bar{d} - 2s\bar{s})$. The flavor-singlet state is:

$$|\eta_0 \rangle = \frac{1}{\sqrt{3}} (|u\bar{u}\rangle + |d\bar{d}\rangle + |s\bar{s}\rangle)$$

If we project the $\pi^0$ or $\eta_8$ onto our completely symmetric mass operator $(\bar{u}u + \bar{d}d + \bar{s}s)$, the opposing signs cancel out perfectly to zero. The instanton gives exactly **zero** mass to the octet Goldstone bosons.

However, projecting the singlet state $\eta_0$ onto the symmetric mass operator yields exactly $\eta_0^2$. Therefore, the topological tunneling of the vacuum exclusively isolates the flavor-singlet state, generating a massive shift $\Delta m_0^2$ solely for the $\eta_0$ (which is the primary component of the physical $\eta^\prime$ meson).

11. Large-$N_c$ and the Witten-Veneziano Formula

In the large-$N_c$ limit, the mass generation is formalized rigorously by the Witten-Veneziano formula, tying the pure Yang-Mills topological susceptibility $\chi_t$ to the mass of the $\eta^\prime$:

$$m_{\eta^\prime}^2 = m_\eta^2 + \frac{2 N_f}{f_\pi^2} \chi_t$$

Lattice calculations ($\chi_t \approx (180 \text{ MeV})^4$) perfectly account for the heavy mass of the $\eta^\prime$ ($958 \text{ MeV}$), resolving the $U(1)_A$ problem.

12. Flavor Symmetry Breaking and $\eta-\eta^\prime$ Mixing

In an idealized universe where all three light quarks have exactly the same mass ($m_u = m_d = m_s$), $SU(3)$ flavor symmetry is exact. In this limit, the pure octet state $|\eta_8\rangle$ and the pure singlet state $|\eta_0\rangle$ would not interact with each other.

The Mass Matrix

However, in reality, the strange quark is significantly heavier than the up and down quarks ($m_s \gg m_{u,d}$). This explicitly breaks the $SU(3)$ flavor symmetry. Because both the $\eta_8$ and the $\eta_0$ have identical quantum numbers ($J^{PC} = 0^{-+}$), this symmetry breaking forces them to mix.

We can construct a $2 \times 2$ mass squared matrix in the $(|\eta_8\rangle, |\eta_0\rangle)$ basis. The diagonal entries are the bare masses of the states. Using the GMOR relations, the mass of the pure octet is $M_{88}^2 = \frac{4}{3}m_K^2 - \frac{1}{3}m_\pi^2$. The bare mass of the singlet includes both its GMOR quark-mass contribution and the anomalous instanton contribution ($\Delta m_0^2$):

$$M^2 = \begin{pmatrix} M_{88}^2 & M_{80}^2 \\ M_{08}^2 & M_{00}^2 \end{pmatrix} = \begin{pmatrix} \frac{4}{3}m_K^2 - \frac{1}{3}m_\pi^2 & -\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}(m_K^2 - m_\pi^2) \\ -\frac{2\sqrt{2}}{3}(m_K^2 - m_\pi^2) & \frac{2}{3}m_K^2 + \frac{1}{3}m_\pi^2 + \Delta m_0^2 \end{pmatrix}$$

The Off-Diagonal Mixing Terms

Notice the off-diagonal terms $M_{80}^2$ and $M_{08}^2$. They are proportional to $(m_K^2 - m_\pi^2)$. Because $m_K \propto m_s$ and $m_\pi \propto m_{u,d}$, if the strange quark mass were equal to the up/down masses, $m_K$ would equal $m_\pi$, the off-diagonal terms would vanish entirely, and there would be no mixing! It is exclusively the $SU(3)$ explicit symmetry breaking that couples the two states.

Diagonalization and the Physical States

To find the actual, physical particles that hit our detectors, we must diagonalize this matrix. This rotates the mathematical $|\eta_8\rangle$ and $|\eta_0\rangle$ states into the physical $|\eta\rangle$ and $|\eta^\prime\rangle$ states via a mixing angle $\theta_P$:

$$|\eta\rangle = \cos\theta_P |\eta_8\rangle - \sin\theta_P |\eta_0\rangle$$ $$|\eta^\prime\rangle = \sin\theta_P |\eta_8\rangle + \cos\theta_P |\eta_0\rangle$$

Phenomenological fits to experimental decay data dictate that this mixing angle is relatively small, roughly $\theta_P \approx -15^\circ$. Therefore, the physical $\eta$ particle is predominantly the $SU(3)$ octet Goldstone boson, while the physical $\eta^\prime$ particle is predominantly the massive, anomalous $U(1)_A$ singlet.

Summary: The mass of the proton is generated by the strong force (confinement). The tiny masses of the pions are governed by explicit chiral symmetry breaking via the Higgs mechanism. But the heavy mass of the $\eta^\prime$ is generated purely by the topological quantum tunneling (instantons) of the QCD vacuum itself!
